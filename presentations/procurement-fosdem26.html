Shifting from "Free Assets" to "Living Infrastructure"

The Hook

We spend hours debating how to get a $5/month donation from a user on GitHub Sponsors. Meanwhile, governments spend trillions on IT contracts annually. We are looking for funding in the wrong pocket.

Deliver this in the opening minute to set contrast and urgency.

The Problem: The Charity Model Is Broken

  • Relying on donations leads to maintainer burnout and fragile projects.
  • Unfunded maintenance creates insecure supply chains (examples: Log4j, xz-utils).
  • Donations don't scale to meet the maintenance needs of infrastructure.
Mention Log4j and xz-utils as concrete supply-chain examples; keep descriptions short and factual.

The Contract Hack

Slide-ready RFP Clause

RFP clause (concise): Vendor must allocate effort in scope to upstream contribution. All non-proprietary fixes required by this contract shall be submitted to the relevant upstream projects within 30 days and tracked in public issue trackers.

Acceptance Criteria (bullets)

  • Vendor shows upstream contribution history (links to commits/prs).
  • All applicable fixes submitted upstream within 30 days of delivery.
  • Security fixes have a disclosure and remediation timeline agreed in contract.
  • Evidence: ACR/VPAT-style report or repository links provided at acceptance.

Reference: Drupal Association RFP Guide

Explain why upstream-first clauses reduce forked risk and improve velocity; keep the clause short and enforceable. Use acceptance criteria during evaluation and contract award.

DITAP & Procurement Culture

  • Procurement officers fear risk
  • Open standards reduce lock-in risk
  • Resource: U.S. Digital Service (USDS) Digital IT Acquisition Playbook (DITAP) curriculum
Invite procurement teams to DITAP; stress risk framing and open standards.

  • Maintainers: build enterprise-ready docs
