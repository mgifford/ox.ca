  • Unfunded maintenance creates insecure supply chains (examples: Log4j, xz-utils).
  • Donations don't scale to meet the maintenance needs of infrastructure
  • Infstructure needs ongoing, reliable investments

OSS needs ongoing care: not 1-time gift

  • Many companies are moving from one-time project budgets to annual service contracts
  • Procurement shifted from 'buying software' to 'funding operational support'
  • Encourage recurring revenue for maintainers: support, security & upgrades
  • Obviously with Free Software, this is just a metaphor
Talk point: Many vendors moved from perpetual licenses to subscription/maintenance models. Procurement can mirror that shift for open source by budgeting ongoing maintenance & support — this sustains upstream projects & reduces technical debt.

The Contract Hack 1

Slide-ready RFP Clause

RFP clause (concise): Vendor must allocate effort in scope to upstream contribution. All non-proprietary fixes required by this contract shall be submitted to the relevant upstream projects within 30 days & tracked in public issue trackers.

Acceptance Criteria (bullets)

  • Vendor shows upstream contribution history (links to commits/prs).
  • All applicable fixes submitted upstream within 30 days of delivery.
  • Security fixes have a disclosure & remediation timeline agreed in contract.
  • Evidence: ACR/VPAT-style report or repository links provided at acceptance.

Reference: Drupal Association RFP Guide

Explain why upstream-first clauses reduce forked risk & improve velocity; keep the clause short & enforceable. Use acceptance criteria during evaluation & contract award.

The Contract Hack 2

Acceptance Criteria (bullets)

  • All deliverables must be 100% compliant with open standards
  • All code needs to be given to the client, who needs to be able to use, modify & distriute it without restriction
  • You don't have to say anything about licenses

The Contract Hack 3

Working in the open

  • Public repository must be created & linked in deliverables before final acceptance
  • Repository must include license, README, contribution guide, and CI checks (security/accessibility where applicable)
  • Project management & issue queues should be done in the open by default
  • Optional enforcement: small payment holdback released after repository verification or upstream merge
Talk: Require the public repo early — full git history enables audits, provenance, and long-term reuse. Require license and CI; use holdbacks or escrow only when publication alone isn't sufficient to guarantee maintenance.

Flow: Gov ($$) → Agency (hires) → Maintainer (code) → Foundation (governance) → Better software → Gov benefit. Use simple arrows or SVG if desired; ensure text alt. Describe the loop verbally; emphasize reinvestment & governance.

Call to Action

  • Maintainers: build enterprise-ready docs
  • Agencies: sell resilience, not hours
  • Government: fund capabilities, not one-off projects
  • The 1% Pledge: allocate 1% of contract value to upstream maintenance & security of dependencies.
Short, actionable asks mapped to each actor. Thesis reminder: procurement funding sustains maintenance — donations alone won't scale.

Procurement is often bureaucratic & overlooked — it is also the most powerful lever to sustain the Open Web.

  • Procurement controls where public money flows — fund open deliverables to sustain upstream projects
  • Recurring contracts create predictable maintenance revenue, reducing supply-chain risk
  • Open deliverables increase reuse, transparency, and long-term sovereignty
  • Small policy changes (RFP clauses, holdbacks, 1% pledges) scale into large, sustained investments
Delivery: Close by linking the thesis — donations aren't enough, procurement is where budgets and governance meet code. Call to action: adopt one procurement change this year (e.g., 1% pledge or public repo requirement).
