Hook: June 28, 2025 is in the rear-view mirror. If you shipped this morning without WCAG 2.2 checks and service-wide support coverage, you may already be out of step with where EN 301 549 is heading.

Accessibility in the EU

  • Government lead the way with the Web Accessibility Directive (WAD).
  • To do this a new EU standard needed was built, EN 301 549
  • EN 301 549 is an evolving standard based on the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C)'s Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)
  • The next release is expected for early 2026 and on the web should mostly align with WCAG 2.2 AA

WAD set the public-sector floor (Directive (EU) 2016/2102). EN 301 549 is the procurement-ready wrapper that turns WCAG into an ICT-wide standard (web, apps, docs, software, support). WCAG remains the normative source for web requirements via EN 301 549’s web clauses (notably the WCAG-mapping section). WCAG 2.2 is already a W3C Recommendation; EN 301 549 revisions are expected to track it in the next cycle, so treat WCAG 2.2 as the direction of travel now, not a future surprise. Emphasize “service-wide compliance”: web + documents + mobile + support channels + procurement, not just homepage scores. References: WAD (EUR-Lex) https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2016/2102/oj/eng ; WCAG 2.2 https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/ ; EN 301 549 library entry https://accessible-eu-centre.ec.europa.eu/content-corner/digital-library/en-3015492021-accessibility-requirements-ict-products-and-services_en

The European Accessibility Act (EAA) & All Things Digital

  • EAA (Directive 2019/882) extends beyond public sector
  • It became law on June 28, 2025; 27 member states = 27 enforcements
  • Fines can reach up to €1M — authorities may monitor compliance
  • Some jurisdictions provide some microenterprise relief; you may not be exempt
  • If your Drupal site sells a ticket but your help desk is inaccessible, you fail

EAA (Directive (EU) 2019/882) moves accessibility from “public sector obligation” to “single market requirement” for defined products and services (Annex I). The practical trap: teams focus on the website UI and ignore the service boundary. Under EN 301 549 logic, the user’s journey includes PDFs, email flows, identity and payments, call centers, chat, kiosks, and support scripts. June 28, 2025 is the application date, but enforcement is national: different authorities, different complaint routes, different penalties, different priorities. Treat exemptions as narrow and document-driven, not hand-wavy. References: EAA (EUR-Lex) https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2019/882/oj/eng ; AccessibleEU centre https://accessible-eu-centre.ec.europa.eu ; EN 301 549 entry https://accessible-eu-centre.ec.europa.eu/content-corner/digital-library/en-3015492021-accessibility-requirements-ict-products-and-services_en

Web Almanac 2025 — The Hard Truth

  • Lighthouse scores trend up as automation gets easier
  • Automated tests only cover ~1/3 of accessibility issues
  • Only ~29% of mobile sites pass the Lighthouse color-contrast audit
  • The draft 2025 Almanac signals modest movement, not a step-change

Use Web Almanac data to puncture false confidence. Automated scores improve year over year, but core usability failures remain routine. In the published 2024 Accessibility chapter, the Lighthouse “color-contrast” audit passes on 29% of mobile pages, up from 23% in 2022. That is progress, but it is still failure at internet scale. This is Goodhart’s Law in practice: teams optimize what tools measure. Automation finds patterns; it does not prove task success, comprehension, or low-friction journeys. Label 2025 as draft/preliminary and do not oversell small deltas. References: Web Almanac 2024 Accessibility (Color Contrast + improved audits) https://almanac.httparchive.org/en/2024/accessibility ; Lighthouse overview https://developer.chrome.com/docs/lighthouse/overview/

WCAG 2.2 AA: The Practical Upgrade

Better accessibility by removing cognitive barriers to login, making help predictable, improving touch accuracy, and ensuring keyboard focus not lost.


  • Accessible authentication (3.3.8 AA)
  • Consistent help (3.2.6 A)
  • Target size minimum (2.5.8 AA)
  • Focus not obscured (2.4.11 AA)

Keep this slide concrete: WCAG 2.2 is not abstract. It hits real pain points people complain about. 3.3.8 targets “memory tests” and fragile authentication flows; it matters for MFA, password managers, and cognitive load. 3.2.6 stops “help whiplash” by making support predictable. 2.5.8 makes small touch targets less error-prone. 2.4.11 is a direct strike at sticky headers, cookie banners, chat widgets, and modals that hide focus. References (exact SC text): 3.3.8 https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/#accessible-authentication-minimum ; 3.2.6 https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/#consistent-help ; 2.5.8 https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/#target-size-minimum ; 2.4.11 https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/#focus-not-obscured-minimum

EAA Reporting

Publish It, Maintain It, Produce It on Request.


  • WAD explicitly mandates an Accessibility Statement model (public sector)
  • EAA mandates service accessibility information (Annex V): public, accessible, maintained
  • Authorities and customers will ask for evidence, not promises
  • Practical output: a publish information on services needing to meet A11y requirements
  • Keep a living defect and remediation log (our issue queue)<

Be precise about terminology. WAD (Directive (EU) 2016/2102) explicitly requires an accessibility statement and the Commission published a model statement (Implementing Decision (EU) 2018/1523). EAA does not reuse the phrase “accessibility statement”, but it does require something functionally similar for services: Annex V requires service providers to make accessibility information public, in accessible formats, and to keep it updated and available as long as the service is offered. This is the part most teams miss. The compliance deliverable is not just “fix bugs”; it is “publish the accessibility information, keep it current, and be ready to produce supporting evidence on request.” References: WAD https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2016/2102/oj/eng ; model statement decision https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dec_impl/2018/1523/oj/eng ; EAA Annex V https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2019/882/oj/eng (see Annex V) ; TetraLogical understanding document https://tetralogical.com/blog/2025/03/19/understanding-the-eaa/

Drupal Is Already a Strong Baseline

  • Core culture: accessibility issues are treated as defects, not optional enhancements
  • Modern Drupal is built for semantic HTML, keyboard access, and accessible theming patterns
  • Community commitment: track WCAG evolution (including WCAG 2.2 AA)
  • Risk warning: you can still break accessibility with themes, content, and integrations

This is the “don’t panic” slide. After standards and reporting requirements, decision-makers need to hear: you are not starting from zero. Drupal has an active accessibility team and governance expectations that track the current WCAG Recommendation, including WCAG 2.2. The message is not “Drupal guarantees compliance.” The message is “Drupal reduces the baseline risk if you do not sabotage it with bad themes, inaccessible components, PDFs-as-a-strategy, and third-party widgets.” Reference: Drupal accessibility feature page https://www.drupal.org/about/features/accessibility

LocalGov Drupal — Collective Action on Accessibility

  • LGD targets WCAG 2.2 AA out of the box; shared patterns reduce per-council risk
  • Example: Southwark PDF Importer turns inaccessible PDFs into accessible HTML for everyone
  • Broader Drupal community: contribute accessibility fixes upstream to core and contrib modules
  • Shared governance means faster remediation aligned with evolving standards (EN 301 549, WCAG updates)

Position LGD as “how public sector executes compliance at scale.” Shared patterns and shared governance reduce duplication, reduce regressions, and speed up remediation. The Southwark PDF Importer is the right kind of innovation: shifting from PDF-first publishing to HTML-first, which is usually more accessible and more maintainable. Tie it back to EN 301 549 service-wide thinking: content, templates, workflows, and support all matter. References: LGD accessibility target conformance (WCAG 2.2 AA) https://docs.localgovdrupal.org/accessibility/ ; Southwark PDF Importer project write-up https://www.drupal.org/about/starshot/initiatives/ai/blog/from-hours-to-minutes-building-an-ai-powered-pdf-importer-for-local-government-for-localgov-drupal ; LGD main site https://localgovdrupal.org

EN 301 549 — The European Wrapper

  • Legal benchmark for WAD and beyond; increasingly reused as WCAG-plus for procurement
  • Today aligns to WCAG 2.1 AA; next revision is expected to reference WCAG 2.2 — start migrating now
  • Clause 11.7 (user preferences) means respecting browser/OS settings and offering personalization (light/dark, reduced motion)

Frame EN 301 549 as the procurement touchstone: it incorporates WCAG for web content and extends beyond it to software, documents, support, and operational requirements. The reason to “start migrating now” is simple: if you write procurement language that hard-codes WCAG 2.1 AA today, you are purchasing remediation debt the moment EN updates. Use clause-style thinking: user preferences (11.7) forces you to stop overriding OS/browser settings and to design for reduced motion and color scheme preferences. References: EN 301 549 library entry https://accessible-eu-centre.ec.europa.eu/content-corner/digital-library/en-3015492021-accessibility-requirements-ict-products-and-services_en ; WCAG 2.2 https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/

AI & Accessibility — Friend or Foe?

  • Use AI for drafts: alt-text suggestions, structure checks
  • Never outsource final judgment; humans feel friction, AI does not
  • Keep data local; respect privacy and procurement constraints

Frame AI as an accelerator for competent teams, not a compliance engine. Good uses: draft alt text (with human review), detect structural issues (headings/labels), assist with remediation triage. Bad uses: “AI will make it compliant.” AI does not experience confusion, vestibular issues, low vision, cognitive load, or the stress of a broken form. It will hallucinate plausible explanations and confident nonsense. Keep the governance message blunt: treat AI as a data handling and procurement decision. Prefer local or controlled deployments where possible; avoid leaking content, user data, or incident details. Caution hard on overlays and “automated remediation” products that promise compliance. References: Web Almanac overlay section (automation limits) https://almanac.httparchive.org/en/2024/accessibility ; IAAP overlay position is cited in that chapter; additional EU-facing commentary exists (example) https://www.fondazionelia.org/en/research-and-development/accessibility-overlays-are-not-the-solution-says-the-european-commission/

Strategic Procurement (EN 301 549)

  • Mandate Annex V service accessibility info (publishable) and WAD-style statements where applicable
  • Stop buying debt: require an ACR/VPAT-style evidence package mapped to EN 301 549 before award
  • Mandate the LGD Pattern Library; no bespoke bugs on public money
  • Ask for upstream history, not just "we know accessibility"

Make procurement do the work. EN 301 549 only matters if you enforce it at contract award and acceptance, not after launch. Require two outputs: (1) a publishable accessibility information page aligned with EAA Annex V (services) and WAD statement requirements where relevant, and (2) an evidence package that procurement can evaluate (test reports, known issues, remediation plan). ACR/VPAT is a practical reporting format for suppliers because it has an EN 301 549 edition and forces structured disclosure. Mandate shared patterns (LGD Pattern Library) so councils do not pay repeatedly for the same errors. “Upstream history” means evidence of contributions and maintenance, not claims. References: EAA Annex V https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2019/882/oj/eng ; ITI VPAT/ACR overview https://www.itic.org/policy/accessibility/vpat ; LGD patterns https://patterns.localgovdrupal.org

What to Do Monday — For Businesses Serving Europeans

  • Audit your services against WCAG 2.2 AA; document gaps and known issues
  • Publish and maintain your EAA Annex V service accessibility information
  • If you are public sector (or delivering public sector services), update your WAD accessibility statement
  • Update procurement: require EN 301 549 evidence, not “we know accessibility”
  • Check national transposition and enforcement routes; do not assume uniformity

Make this actionable and non-theoretical. Step 1 is an audit, but the output is documentation: known issues + remediation plan. Step 2 is publishing: Annex V service accessibility information must be public and accessible, and it must be kept current. Step 3 is WAD hygiene where applicable: accessibility statement + feedback mechanism + enforcement link. Step 4 is procurement alignment: make suppliers show EN 301 549 evidence early. Step 5 is jurisdiction reality: enforcement is national.
